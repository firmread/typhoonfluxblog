The goal is to find out what this space is all about and what it’s intentions are.

Background
The High Line was built in the 1930s as part of a public-private infrastructure project called the West Side Improvement which aimed to better transport goods along 10th Avenue nicknamed Death Avenue via an elevated train system. The tracks were closed down in 1980 and were not in use again until 2009 when the High Line was reopened as a public park.
A non-profit organization called Friends of the High Line rallied to preserve the site, however their initial vision was not to make a park, but to maintain the structure as part of the neighborhood. It was not common knowledge that since the railways closed a prosperous urban wildscape existed. It was only accessible through buildings that bordered the tracks, and people would climb up the fire escape to reach the top of the High Line.

Observation
A survey of the 10th Avenue Square was taken broken out by gender and estimated age as well as their physical presence in the theatre area for each hour throughout the course of the day. This data set will allow us to see what times this area is busiest and what kinds of people are likely to be present at any given time period.

Impression
After having interviewed visitors, neighborhood locals, and workers at the High Line, it has become apparent that this space is rapidly growing and changing. It was also valuable to hear different sets of impressions about the space from a wide variety of people.




Behavior
There was a noticeable difference between people’s overall behaviors during the day compared to night. There seemed to be more interaction with different aspects of the park during the day in comparison to night. Most people did not stop to sit down or observe the park after 7pm. The audio clip provides us with the sounds we can expect during the day, which is generally loud, but without the sounds of traffic.

Patterns
Throughout the day patterns began to develop in the theatre space. Typically people would walk down to the bottom and take pictures in front of the glass then sit on the benches for a short period of time and talk. Our time lapse camera seemed to have an effect on people, and we noticed a slight change in their behavior once they acknowledged they were being filmed.

 

Surprises
We unexpectedly came across a street performance by a Brazilian artist Michel Melamed has been performing a play called “SEEWATCHLOOK”, which is a compilation of short skits performed by 12 actors at the street intersection below 10th Avenue Square. It was very helpful to witness this super cool example of an intervention in this space.

Experience

Firm –

  • I think it’s interesting how the children like to run down the ramp and keep climbing up back to their parents.
  • I think that in the evening people not really sit on the back of the theater that much because it’s really windy when it’s getting dark.
  • When it’s dark people tends to need more privacy, they don’t cooperate with the interview as much as in the daytime.

Paul –

  • I noticed a lot of families enjoying the space, interatcing with each other and having a great time together.

Tom –

  • During the day people walk down the stairs to the front of the windows and take pictures and sit, but at night they take pictures from the top of the stairs and continue walking.
  • The street sign on the corner of 10th Ave and 17th Street is lit up.
  • The park is also used by select street musicians and vendors with special permission from the park and permits from the city.

Yong –

  • I think it’s interesting to see people’s impressions of the space which they described as ‘Aquarium’, ‘Amazing’, ‘Really Cool’, ‘Watching cars on the street like a movie’, ‘Great’.
  • People bring food to eat, chat with friends and enjoy the sun, and love to watch 10th Ave through the window.
  • Met with some architects who discussed the space and said they were there to study and learn about the space.
